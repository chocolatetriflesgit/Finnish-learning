A complete self-study course: 24 lessons in five modules, taking you from your first "hei" to reading simple news texts about work, housing and the economy. All fifteen grammatical cases are covered, six verb types, four tenses, and roughly 600 words of vocabulary. By the end you should be solidly at CEFR A2, with a foot in the door of B1.

How to use this course

Work through the lessons in order — Finnish grammar is cumulative and later lessons assume everything before them. A realistic pace is two to three lessons a week at 45–60 minutes each, which puts the whole course at three to four months. Do every exercise on paper or aloud before revealing the answers — each exercise has a collapsible Vastaukset (answers) panel directly beneath it, and Appendix F keeps the full key in one place for printing. Say everything out loud: Finnish spelling is completely phonetic, so reading aloud is pronunciation practice.

Two habits will multiply your progress. First, put every vocabulary table into a spaced-repetition app (Anki or similar) and review daily. Second, from Module 2 onwards, listen to Yle Uutiset selkosuomeksi (Yle's news in simplified Finnish, free online with text and audio) — even five minutes a day trains your ear.

A note on registers: written/standard Finnish (kirjakieli) and everyday spoken Finnish (puhekieli) differ noticeably. This course teaches standard Finnish first because all grammar builds on it, then gives you a full spoken-language survival kit in Lesson 19. Don't panic when real Finns sound different from your textbook — Lesson 19 explains exactly how.

Lesson 0 — Sounds and spelling (Ääntäminen)

Finnish is one of the most learner-friendly languages in the world to pronounce: one letter, one sound, always. If you can spell it, you can say it. Master this lesson properly and you will never need pronunciation guides again.

The vowels

Letter Sounds like Example
a "a" in father (short) kala — fish
e "e" in pet meno — going
i "i" in pin kivi — stone
o "o" in pot (pure, no glide) talo — house
u "oo" in put kuka — who
y German ü / French tu (say "ee" with rounded lips) kyllä — yes
ä "a" in cat äiti — mother
ö German ö (say "e" with rounded lips) söi — ate

The consonants are close to English with three notes: j is always English "y" (ja = "ya"), r is rolled, and h is always pronounced, even before consonants (vihko). The letters b, c, f, q, w, x, z appear only in loanwords.

Length is everything

Double letters are held roughly twice as long, and length changes meaning:

  • tuli (fire) — tuuli (wind) — tulli (customs)
  • muta (mud) — muuta (other, partitive) — mutta (but)
  • tapaan (I meet) — tapan (I kill). Worth getting right before meeting anyone.

Stress

Always, without exception, on the first syllable. HEL-sin-ki, MU-sta-mä-ki, RA-vin-to-la.

Vowel harmony

Finnish words contain either back vowels (a, o, u) or front vowels (ä, ö, y) — never both (e and i are neutral and mix with either). Every suffix therefore comes in two flavours and you pick the one that matches the word:

Suffix Back word Front word
-ssa / -ssä (in) talossa (in the house) metsässä (in the forest)
-ko / -kö (question) onko? (is it?) syökö? (does he eat?)
-lla / -llä (on) pöydällä? no — pöytä is front: pöydällä has ä because of ä in pöytä tuolilla (on the chair — i and u… u is back, so -lla)

Rule of thumb: scan the word for a, o or u. If you find one, use the a-form of the suffix; otherwise the ä-form. Compound words harmonise by their last part: kirjakauppa → kirjakaupassa.

Diphthongs

Vowel pairs pronounced as a glide within one syllable: ai, ei, oi, ui, yi, äi, öi, au, ou, eu, iu, äy, öy, ie, uo, yö. So sauna = SOW-na (and yes, sauna is the one word Finnish has given English).

Notes

Finnish has no single word for "please." Politeness lives in kiitos, in tone, and later in the conditional mood (Lesson 15's saisinko — "could I get"). Lesson 2 — Who are you? (olla + pronouns)

The pronouns and the verb olla (to be)

Pronoun olla negative
minä I olen en ole
sinä you (sg.) olet et ole
hän he/she on ei ole
me we olemme emme ole
te you (pl. / polite) olette ette ole
he they ovat eivät ole

Three things to absorb early. Hän is gender-neutral — Finnish has no he/she distinction at all. Se (it) and ne (they, things) are used for objects — and, in spoken Finnish, for people too, which is not rude (Lesson 19). And because verb endings show the person, minä/sinä are often dropped: Olen Anna is complete.

Negation uses a negative verb (en, et, ei, emme, ette, eivät) that conjugates, followed by the bare verb: en ole — I am not.

Questions

Yes/no questions attach -ko/-kö to the verb, which moves to the front: Oletko sinä Anna? — Are you Anna? Onko hän suomalainen? — Is he/she Finnish? Answer with the verb: Olen. / En ole. (Finns answer yes/no questions by repeating the verb rather than with a bare kyllä.)

Lesson 3 — First verbs (Verb type 1)

Finnish verbs fall into six types by their infinitive ending. Type 1 — infinitives in vowel + a/ä — is by far the biggest. Remove the final -a/-ä to get the stem, then add personal endings:

puhua (speak) asua (live) kysyä (ask)
minä puhun asun kysyn
sinä puhut asut kysyt
hän puhuu asuu kysyy
me puhumme asumme kysymme
te puhutte asutte kysytte
he puhuvat asuvat kysyvät

The hän-form doubles the stem's final vowel; the he-form obeys vowel harmony (-vat/-vät). Negation: negative verb + bare stem — en puhu, et asu, hän ei kysy, emme puhu.

ostaa to buy katsoa to watch, look
rakastaa to love odottaa* to wait

The starred verbs shift consonants when conjugated (luen, odotan, kirjoitan, annan, otan, ymmärrän, tiedän, autan) — this is consonant gradation, the single most important sound rule in Finnish, and Lesson 5 is devoted to it. For now just learn these first-person forms as given.

Word order is flexible but neutral order is Subject–Verb–Object, and Finnish has no articles — ostan auton covers both "I buy a car" and "I buy the car."

Numbers trigger the partitive

Here is your first real taste of Finland's favourite case. After any number greater than one, the noun goes in the partitive singular:

yksi kahvi → kaksi kahvia → kolme olutta → viisi euroa (the partitive ending in bold: kahvia, olutta, euroa)

Why "two of coffee"? The partitive expresses an amount of something. You'll get the full picture in Lesson 8; for now, apply the pattern to money and ordering: kolme euroa viisikymmentä (senttiä) = €3.50. Note Finnish uses a decimal comma: 3,50 €.

Lesson 5 — Consonant gradation (Astevaihtelu)

Before the cases, one sound law. When you attach most endings to a Finnish word, the consonants k, p, t in the last syllable often weaken. This is consonant gradation, and it explains why kauppa (shop) becomes kaupassa (in the shop). Learn the pattern table cold — it applies to thousands of nouns and verbs.

Strong Weak Example (basic form → genitive)
kk k kukka → kukan (flower)
pp p kauppa → kaupan (shop)
tt t tyttö → tytön (girl)
k – (disappears) jalka → jalan (foot, leg)
p v leipä → leivän (bread)
t d katu → kadun (street)
nk ng Helsinki → Helsingin
nt nn Irlanti → Irlannin
mp mm lampi → lammen (pond)
lt ll ilta → illan (evening)
rt rr parta → parran (beard)

When does the weak grade appear? The working rule: when the ending closes the syllable — which covers the genitive -n and most case endings you're about to learn (-ssa, -sta, -lla, -lta, -lle). The strong grade survives in the basic form and in the partitive, illative and essive cases: kauppa, kauppaa, kauppaan, kauppana versus kaupan, kaupassa, kaupalla.

Some words gradate in reverse — weak in the basic form, strong with endings: hammas → hampaan (tooth), opas → oppaan (guide). And clusters like sk, st, tk don't gradate at all: matka → matkan (trip), posti → postin.

You already met gradation in verbs: type 1 verbs show the strong grade in the infinitive and the hän/he forms, weak elsewhere — lukea: luen, luet, lukee, luemme, luette, lukevat. That's why tiedän but hän tietää.

Lesson 6 — Where? Where from? Where to? (The six location cases)

Finnish replaces prepositions like in, from, to with case endings, arranged in a beautifully logical 3×2 grid — inside something versus on/at something, crossed with being there, leaving, and going:

Interior ("in") Exterior ("on/at")
Being (missä? where?) inessive -ssa/-ssä — talossa (in the house) adessive -lla/-llä — pöydällä (on the table)
Leaving (mistä? from where?) elative -sta/-stä — talosta (out of the house) ablative -lta/-ltä — pöydältä (off the table)
Entering (mihin? where to?) illative -Vn/-hVn/-seen — taloon (into the house) allative -lle — pöydälle (onto the table)

The illative is the only tricky formation: short final vowel doubles + n (talo → taloon, Helsinki → Helsinkiin); after a long vowel or diphthong, -hVn echoing the vowel (maa → maahan, työ → työhön); words like huone take -seen (huoneeseen). Remember from Lesson 5: partitive, illative and essive keep the strong grade — kauppaan but kaupassa.

The exterior cases also carry other meanings you'll meet constantly: the adessive marks instruments and transport (bussilla — by bus, kortilla — by card, kirjoitan kynällä — I write with a pen), the ablative marks asking/receiving from a person (kysyn häneltä — I ask him/her), and the allative giving to a person (annan sinulle — I give to you).

"I have": minulla on

Finnish has no verb to have. Instead, the owner goes in the adessive and the thing owned is the subject: literally "on me is."

Minulla on koira. — I have a dog. · Sinulla on kaunis asunto. — You have a beautiful flat. · Onko teillä lapsia? — Do you have children? · Kenellä on avain? — Who has the key?

The negative takes the partitive (Lesson 8 explains why): Minulla ei ole autoa. — I don't have a car.

Possessive suffixes

Standard Finnish doubles up possession: pronoun in genitive plus a suffix on the noun — minun kirjani (my book), sinun nimesi, hänen autonsa, meidän talomme, teidän koiranne, heidän lapsensa. In everyday speech the suffix is usually dropped (mun kirja), and with third parties the genitive alone appears: Annan kirja. Recognise the suffixes; produce them when writing formally.

Lesson 8 — The partitive: Finland's favourite case

If Finnish grammar has a boss level, this is it — but it yields to patterns. The partitive expresses an unspecified amount of something, or an action that doesn't run to completion.

Formation

Ending When Examples
-a / -ä after a short final vowel kahvi → kahvia, talo → taloa, kauppa → kauppaa
-ta / -tä after a long vowel/diphthong, or a consonant stem maa → maata, työ → työtä, mies → miestä, kieli → kieltä, pieni → pientä
-tta / -ttä e-words (huone type) huone → huonetta, perhe → perhettä

Watch the old i-words, which use consonant stems: vesi → vettä, käsi → kättä, lapsi → lasta, uusi → uutta. And remember: the partitive keeps the strong grade (kauppaa, not kaupaa).

The five core uses

  1. After numbers (Lesson 4): kolme kahvia, viisi euroa, kaksi lasta.
  2. After negation: Minulla ei ole autoa. En juo kahvia. Emme osta leipää. Negative sentences take partitive objects, full stop.
  3. Indefinite amounts / mass nouns: Juon kahvia (I drink [some] coffee) versus Juon kahvin (I drink the/a whole coffee). Ostan maitoa ja leipää.
  4. Ongoing, incomplete action: Luen kirjaa — I'm reading a book (in progress). Lesson 13 develops this into the full object system.
  5. After certain verbs, always: rakastaa (rakastan sinua), odottaa (odotan bussia), harrastaa (harrastan uintia — I do swimming), opiskella (opiskelen suomea), pelata (pelaan jalkapalloa), ajatella (ajattelen sinua — I'm thinking of you).

Bonus: those greetings from Lesson 1 — hyvää päivää, hyvää yötä — are partitives. You're literally wishing someone "[some] good day."

Telling the time

Paljonko kello on? — What time is it? Kello on kolme. — It's three. Half-hours count towards the coming hour: puoli kaksi = half past ONE (1:30) — the classic trap for English speakers. Viittätoista vaille neljä — quarter to four; kymmenen yli kaksi — ten past two (vaille takes the partitive, yli the basic form). In timetables, just read digits: kello 15.30 — viisitoista kolmekymmentä.

Times of day take the adessive: aamulla (in the morning), päivällä, illalla, yöllä. Days of the week take the essive -na for "on": maanantai → maanantaina, tiistai, keskiviikko, torstai, perjantai, lauantai, sunnuntai; viikonloppuna — at the weekend. Frequency adverbs: aina (always), usein (often), joskus (sometimes), harvoin (rarely), ei koskaan (never).

Type Infinitive → participle

Lesson 13 — The object: total or partial?

This is the grammar point Finns themselves will tell you is the hardest — and the most satisfying to crack. Every object in a Finnish sentence is either total (the action covers the whole thing, to completion) or partial (partitive: incomplete, ongoing, indefinite, or negated).

Total object — the action is complete and covers the whole

Form: genitive -n in the singular (Luin kirjan — I read the book, finished it), nominative in the plural (Ostin kengät — I bought the shoes), and nominative also after imperatives and täytyy: Osta kirja! Minun täytyy ostaa liput.

Partial object — partitive

Used when the action is ongoing (Luin kirjaa, kun hän soitti — I was reading the book when he called), negated (En lukenut kirjaa), covers an indefinite amount (Ostin maitoa — I bought some milk), or follows an inherently open-ended verb (rakastaa, odottaa, ajatella, tarvita, harrastaa, etsiä, kuunnella, katsoa in the sense of watching).

Minimal pairs — read these until they feel different

  • Join kahvin. (I drank the coffee — cup emptied.) / Join kahvia. (I drank some coffee.)
  • Luin kirjan eilen. (Read it cover to cover.) / Luin kirjaa eilen. (Did some reading of it.)
  • Ostin asunnon. (I bought a flat — done deal.) / Etsin asuntoa. (I'm looking for a flat.)
  • Rakennan taloa. (I'm building a house — in progress.) / Rakensin talon. (I built the house.)

Personal pronouns have a special accusative

minut, sinut, hänet, meidät, teidät, heidät — and kenet (whom): Hän vei minut lentokentälle. — She took me to the airport. Tunnetko hänet? — Do you know him/her? In the negative, back to partitive: En tunne häntä.

One elegant extra: durations behave like total objects — Asuin Suomessa vuoden. — I lived in Finland for a year.

Adjectives agree with their noun

In case and number, always: iso talo → isossa talossa → isot talot → isoja taloja. You've been doing this quietly since hyvää päivää; now it's official.

Comparative and superlative

Comparative -mpi, superlative -in, both attached to the (inflectional) stem, gradation applying: helppo → helpompi → helpoin (easy); vanha → vanhempi → vanhin; kylmä → kylmempi → kylmin. Two-syllable adjectives ending in -a/-ä change it to -e-: matala → matalampi. Irregulars worth their weight: hyvä → parempi → paras (good/better/best), pitkä → pidempi → pisin (long/tall), uusi → uudempi → uusin. "Than" = kuin: Helsinki on isompi kuin Turku. Lesson 16 — Commands and directions (Imperatiivi)

Forming the imperative

Singular: the present minä-form minus -n — which means weak grade: puhu! tule! mene! syö! ota! odota! anna! Negative: älä + same form: Älä mene! Älä unohda!

Plural / polite: infinitive stem + -kaa/-kää: puhukaa! tulkaa! menkää! syökää! ottakaa! (Note the stem is the infinitive's, so strong grade returns.) Negative: älkää menkö, älkää ottako — recognise it; you'll rarely produce it early on.

Lesson 17 — Going to do things: the MA-infinitive, obligation, and hobbies

The MA-infinitive: movement toward/at/from an activity

Finnish marks what you're going off to do with case endings on a verb form — the third ("MA") infinitive, built on the strong verb stem:

  • -maan/-mään (into the activity): Menen syömään. — I'm going to eat. Tuletko uimaan? — Coming swimming? Menen salille treenaamaan. — I'm off to the gym to train.
  • -massa/-mässä (in the middle of it): Hän on syömässä. — She's (out) eating. Olin juuri lähtemässä. — I was just leaving.
  • -masta/-mästä (coming from it): Tulen juuri treenaamasta. — I've just come from training.

The pattern also follows verbs of starting and learning: Aloitan opiskelemaan? — careful: standard Finnish prefers alan opiskella or rupean opiskelemaan; but Opettelen puhumaan suomea — I'm learning to speak Finnish — is pure MA-infinitive territory.

Obligation: täytyy and pitää

Both mean "must/have to." The person obliged goes in the genitive, the verb stays in the basic infinitive, and any object is nominative (Lesson 13!):

Minun täytyy mennä nyt. — I have to go now. Sinun pitää varata aika. — You need to book an appointment. Meidän täytyy ostaa liput. — We have to buy the tickets.

The negation is its own verb: ei tarvitse — Sinun ei tarvitse tulla. — You don't have to come. (Ei täydy doesn't exist; this trips up everyone.)

Ability: osata vs voida

Osata = to know how (a learned skill): Osaan puhua suomea. Osaatko uida? Voida = to be able / it's possible: Voin auttaa sinua huomenna. En voi tulla tänään. Permission uses saada: Saako täällä pysäköidä? — May one park here?

Lesson 19 — The passive, plurals in cases, and reading the street

The passive: Finnish's "one does"

The Finnish passive is really an impersonal: puhutaan = "one speaks / people speak / is spoken." Formation, present: type 1 takes the (weak) minä-stem + -taan/-tään with the final a/ä turning to e (puhun → puhutaan; annan → annetaan); other types add -daan/-dään or -laan/-nään/-taan straight onto the infinitive (syödään, tullaan, mennään, halutaan, tarvitaan). Past passive: -ttiin/-tiin: puhuttiin, syötiin, mentiin.

Three uses to internalise:

  1. General statements: Suomessa juodaan paljon kahvia. — In Finland, people drink a lot of coffee. Täällä puhutaan ruotsia. — Swedish is spoken here.
  2. Spoken-language we (as met in Lesson 18): Mennään! — Let's go! Otetaanko taksi? — Shall we take a taxi? Nähdään maanantaina!
  3. Signs and notices — see below.

Plurals beyond the basic -t

You know the nominative plural: talo → talot, kauppa → kaupat (weak grade — the -t closes the syllable). In all other cases, the plural marker is -i- slotted before the case ending: taloissa (in the houses), kaupoissa (in the shops), autoilla (by cars), ystäville (to friends). Final vowels shuffle before the i — a → o in many words (kauppa → kaupoissa), e and i drop (suuri → suurissa) — learn the patterns from examples rather than by rule.

The partitive plural runs on the same -i-: taloja, kirjoja, autoja; naisia, miehiä; huoneita. You need it for existence and shopping: Kaupassa on omenoita. — There are apples in the shop. Ostin kukkia. — I bought (some) flowers. The genitive plural (talojen, naisten) exists and is formed variously; recognise it now, produce it later.

"Who/which/that," declining like a noun for its role in the clause: joka (subject), jonka (genitive/total object), jota (partitive), jossa/josta/johon (in/from/into which), jolla etc.; plural jotka, joita, joissa…

Asunto, jonka ostin, on pieni. — The flat that I bought is small. Kaupunki, jossa asun, kasvaa nopeasti. — The city I live in is growing fast. Se on asia, josta puhutaan paljon. — It's a matter that gets talked about a lot. And the conjunction että = "that": Luulen, että vuokra nousee. — I think that the rent will rise. (Finnish always takes the comma before että and joka — a spelling-test favourite.)

Reading text: Tontti omaksi vai vuokralle?

Helsingissä kaupunki omistaa paljon maata. Kaupunki ei aina myy tonttia, jolla talo seisoo, vaan vuokraa sen pitkällä sopimuksella. Asukkaat maksavat kaupungille vuokraa joka vuosi. Kun maan arvo nousee, myös tontinvuokra nousee vähitellen. Asunto, joka seisoo vuokratontilla, on yleensä halvempi ostaa kuin asunto omalla tontilla — mutta kuukausikulut ovat suuremmat. Monet ekonomistit ajattelevat, että järjestelmä toimii vähän kuin maavero: kaupunki saa osan maan arvosta, jonka koko yhteiskunta luo. Toiset ovat eri mieltä ja sanovat, että vuokra tekee asumisesta kallista.

Glossary: vaan = but rather (after a negative); pitkällä sopimuksella = on a long contract; vähitellen = gradually; kuukausikulut = monthly costs; järjestelmä = system; maavero = land tax; luoda = to create (luo = creates); asumisesta = ~about/of living (elative of the -minen noun asuminen, "living/housing" — verbs become nouns with -minen: lukeminen, asuminen).

Comprehension (Harjoitus 22.1): 1. Mitä kaupunki tekee tontille, jos se ei myy sitä? 2. Mitä tapahtuu tontinvuokralle, kun maan arvo nousee? 3. Miksi jotkut ekonomistit vertaavat (compare) järjestelmää maaveroon? Answer in Finnish, one sentence each.

The last three cases — cases 13, 14, 15 of 15

For completeness, the rare trio. You now formally know all fifteen.

  • Abessive -tta/-ttä — "without": rahatta (without money). In practice everyone says ilman rahaa; the abessive survives mostly in set forms like huomaamatta (without noticing).
  • Comitative -ine- — "together with (one's)," always plural-shaped and with a possessive suffix: Presidentti saapui puolisoineen. — The president arrived with their spouse. Newspaper Finnish; recognise it.
  • Instructive -in — "by means of," fossilised in expressions: omin silmin (with one's own eyes), jalan (on foot), kaksin käsin (with both hands).

Appendix C — Consonant gradation quick card

Strong → weak: kk→k, pp→p, tt→t, k→∅, p→v, t→d, nk→ng, nt→nn, mp→mm, lt→ll, rt→rr. Weak grade before endings that close the syllable (genitive and most cases; type 1 verb forms except hän/he).
